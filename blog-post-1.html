What To Do When Your Car Breaks Down in Hawaii

SafetyJune 5, 2026 · By Marcus Kahala

Breaking down on an Oahu road is stressful under any circumstances — but with the right knowledge, you can stay safe, get help fast, and minimize the disruption to your day. As a towing company that has responded to thousands of roadside incidents across Hawaii, we know exactly what makes a breakdown safer or more dangerous. Here's our complete guide.

Step 1: Get to Safety Immediately

Your first priority the moment you sense vehicle trouble — unusual sounds, warning lights, loss of power — is to move your vehicle out of traffic. On the H1 or H2 expressways, this means steering to the right shoulder as smoothly as possible. If your vehicle loses all power while moving, use your remaining momentum to steer off the road. Do not stop in a travel lane unless your vehicle is completely immobilized.

Once on the shoulder, pull as far right as you can — ideally with all four wheels fully off the paved road. Turn on your hazard lights immediately. This is the single most important safety measure.

Step 2: Stay in Your Vehicle on Highways

This is counterintuitive, but studies consistently show that remaining inside your vehicle is safer than standing outside on a highway shoulder. A vehicle, even a stopped one, provides meaningful protection from passing traffic. If you must exit — for a tire change, or if the vehicle feels unsafe to remain in — exit from the passenger side (away from traffic), stay close to the guardrail or as far from traffic as possible, and wear bright clothing if available.

Step 3: Call for Help

Once you're safely stopped, call for assistance. Save (808) 555-0117 in your phone — that's Ohana Towing, available 24/7. When you call, have your location ready. On major highways, look for the mile marker signs — they're the small green signs every mile that tell rescuers exactly where you are. On surface streets, give the nearest cross streets or a notable landmark.

You can also call 911 if you're on a highway and feel unsafe, or if anyone is injured. Hawaii Highway Safety will dispatch assistance and can help alert motorists.

Step 4: Make Yourself Visible

If you have road flares or reflective triangles, place them 100–200 feet behind your vehicle to warn approaching drivers, especially on curves. If you don't have these (and most people don't), keep your hazard lights running and stay in your vehicle. A fully charged phone is your most important emergency tool.

Step 5: Don't Accept Help from Strangers on Highways

While most people who stop are genuinely trying to help, it's safest to call a licensed, insured towing company directly. Keep your doors locked and windows slightly open when someone approaches. It's fine to tell someone "Help is already on the way" — that's often enough to deter bad actors while being polite to genuine Good Samaritans.
